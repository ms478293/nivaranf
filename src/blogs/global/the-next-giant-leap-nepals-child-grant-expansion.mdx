##

Deep in the heart of the Himalayas, a quiet revolution in social welfare is brewing. As Finance Minister Barshaman Pun prepares to unveil Nepal's annual budget on May 28, the nation holds its breath over a decision that could transform the lives of millions of children and reshape the country's economic future.

<div className="flex flex-wrap gap-4" key="0">
  <BlogParagraph>
    <h2>The Stakes: More Than Just Numbers</h2>
    <p>
      <Link href="https://www.hrw.org/news/2023/04/18/joint-letter-finance-minister-expand-nepals-child-grant-program" style={{color:"#eb5834"}}>Nepal's Child Grant program</Link>, launched as a modest initiative to support
      families with young children, stands at a critical juncture. Currently
      serving 25 of Nepal's 77 districts, along with Dalit children nationwide,
      the program faces what experts call a "moment of truth" - expand
      nationwide or risk perpetuating regional inequalities.
    </p>
    <p>
      "This isn't just about monthly payments," explains Dr. Rama Sharma, a
      senior economist at the Nepal Social Welfare Institute. "We're talking
      about fundamentally altering the trajectory of an entire generation. When
      40% of your population is under 18, your child welfare policies
      essentially become your national development strategy."
    </p>
    <h2>A History of Innovation at Risk</h2>
    <h2>The Pioneer's Paradox</h2>
    <p>
      Nepal's journey in social protection tells a story of bold beginnings and
      hesitant follow-through. In the 1990s, the country shocked the region by
      introducing a universal old age allowance - a move that established Nepal
      as South Asia's social protection pioneer.
    </p>
    <p>
      "Nepal showed remarkable foresight three decades ago," notes James Chen, a
      social policy expert at the World Bank. "The question now is whether it
      can summon that same courage for its children."
    </p>
    <h2>The Current Program: Promise and Limitations</h2>
    <p>
      The existing Child Grant provides monthly support of 532 Nepali rupees
      (US$4) to:
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li>Families with children under five in 25 districts</li>
    </ul>
    <ul>
      <li>All Dalit children under five nationwide</li>
    </ul>
    <ul>
      <li>Approximately 1.3 million children total</li>
    </ul>
